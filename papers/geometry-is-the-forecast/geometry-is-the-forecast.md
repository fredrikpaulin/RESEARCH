# Geometry Is the Forecast: A Position for Latent World-Model Planning in Archipelago Sail Navigation

**Fredrik Paulin** — Bageriet (bageriet.ai), Stockholm
*Preprint, v0.3, June 2026. Position paper. Comments welcome.*

## abstract

Point-to-point sailing in confined coastal waters — archipelagos, sounds, fjords — is poorly served by the two research traditions that should own it. Weather routing assumes forecast-grid wind and optimizes over horizons of days; in an archipelago the wind a boat experiences is dominated by sub-grid interactions with local geometry that no forecast resolves. Learned navigation, meanwhile, has converged on latent world models that plan well in mazes and manipulation tasks but has not touched the marine domain, where the environment itself, not the obstacle map, must be predicted. This paper argues that these two failures are the same opportunity: the wind field in confined waters is a systematic, learnable function of geometry, and joint-embedding predictive world models are the right tool to learn it jointly with vessel dynamics, because ranking candidate routes requires predicting consequences, not reconstructing weather. I survey the relevant prior work across latent world-model planning, autonomous sailing, weather routing, terrain-conditioned wind downscaling, and maritime trajectory learning; identify the gap at their intersection; and propose a concrete research program — chart-derived environments, a minimal sail simulator, a JEPA world model with latent MPC, and a fixed benchmark — designed so that a single researcher with two consumer GPUs can produce a falsifiable result. Nautical raster charts are an underexploited asset: their indexed color palettes are semantic segmentation by construction, making environment generation from real coastal geometry nearly free.

## 1 the position

Three claims, in increasing order of contestability.

**Claim 1: archipelago navigation is a world-model problem, not a routing problem.** Classical weather routing — isochrone methods and their descendants, in continuous use since James (1957) and Hagiwara (1989) — solves global route optimization over forecast wind grids and does it well. Commercial sailing routers are isochrone planners with polar diagrams. But the method's contract is that the wind field is *given*. In open water that contract approximately holds. In the Stockholm archipelago it does not: forecast grids at kilometer scale cannot represent the wind shadow behind a 400 m island, the acceleration through a 200 m sound, or the lift along a headland — effects that routinely decide whether a passage takes twenty minutes or ninety. The information that decides the route is not in the input. No improvement to the optimizer fixes an input problem.

**Claim 2: the missing input is learnable, because it is systematic.** Local sailors carry this model in their heads: given a southwesterly at 8 m/s and this chart, they know where it dies and where it funnels. The atmospheric science literature has, in a different setting, already demonstrated that this kind of knowledge is learnable from data: deep networks conditioned on high-resolution terrain reproduce CFD- and NWP-grade microscale wind structure at a small fraction of the computational cost (Wind-Topo, DEVINE, TerraWind, FuXi-CFD — §2.3). Those systems target mountain terrain and wind-energy or snow applications, and they stop at prediction. Nobody has closed the loop from geometry-conditioned wind to *navigation decisions*.

**Claim 3: the right way to close that loop is a latent world model, not a wind-field regressor bolted onto a router.** The decision-relevant question is never "what is the wind everywhere"; it is "if I take the windward channel, what state am I in six minutes from now." Joint-embedding predictive architectures answer that question directly: they predict the future in representation space, conditioned on actions, without reconstructing observations — and recent work shows that planning over such latents transfers and generalizes better from suboptimal offline data than model-free alternatives (§2.1). A latent that is sufficient to rank candidate action sequences does not need to be sufficient to render a weather map. That asymmetry is the efficiency the whole proposal rests on.

## 2 prior work

### 2.1 latent world models and planning

The line of work this proposal extends began with latent dynamics models for control (Ha & Schmidhuber 2018; PlaNet; the Dreamer series) and was reframed by LeCun's JEPA position paper (2022): learn representations by predicting in embedding space, never in pixel space. The variant that matters here is what recent work labels the JEPA world model (JEPA-WM) recipe — a dynamics model trained solely with a predictive loss in embedding space, no reconstruction, no reward or value heads (Terver et al. 2025, "What Drives Success in Physical Planning with Joint-Embedding Predictive World Models?").

Three results anchor the proposal:

- **PLDM** (Sobal et al. 2025; NeurIPS 2025) trains encoder and predictor jointly with variance–covariance regularization on top-down 2D navigation (Two-Room, Diverse Mazes) and plans by optimizing actions to minimize latent distance to an encoded goal. Its headline finding, now peer-reviewed: latent-dynamics planning generalizes to novel layouts, stitches trajectories, and is more data-efficient than goal-conditioned RL baselines, especially from suboptimal reward-free data. Skerry is, deliberately, PLDM's environment class with real coastal geometry and a wind field for walls.
- **DINO-WM and V-JEPA 2-AC** demonstrate the frozen-encoder variant at scale: action-conditioned predictors over pretrained visual latents support zero-shot manipulation via latent MPC. The JEPA-WM study (Terver et al. 2025) systematically compares design choices across these systems and finds, among other things, that sampling-based planners (CEM/MPPI) are robust where gradient-based planners stall in local minima on navigation tasks — directly relevant to planner selection in confined channels.
- The architecture family is acquiring the extensions a stochastic wind field needs: probabilistic and variational JEPA formulations for uncertainty-aware latent prediction (Var-JEPA, Gögl & Yau 2026), value-shaped latent geometry that makes Euclidean latent distance approximate goal-conditioned cost (Destrade et al. 2026), and principled anti-collapse regularization (LeJEPA; SIGReg). My own empirical work on SIGReg/learning-rate interactions in small LeWM training (Paulin 2026) sits in this last thread and supplies the training recipe skerry starts from.

What none of this work touches: environments whose *dynamics field* (as opposed to obstacle geometry) is the object of generalization. In Two-Room, walls are the environment. At sea, the geometry is static and trivially observed; what the model must infer is the spatially varying, geometry-induced forcing — and whether JEPA latents capture a hidden field that modulates dynamics is, to my knowledge, untested.

### 2.2 autonomous sailing and weather routing

Learned path planning for sailboats exists but is thin and architecturally conservative. The representative result is Q-learning over a coarse grid with wind-direction-dependent action sets to respect the no-go zone (Silva Junior et al. 2020, N-Boat) — tabular RL on a hand-coded environment, which subsequent surveys criticize as data-hungry and compute-heavy relative to its payoff. More recent work pairs a learned wind *time-series* predictor with replanning (a temporal self-attention network forecasting wind along the route, 2025) or improves classical search with sailing-aware heuristics and ant-colony/heuristic hybrids. A 2025 ocean-engineering review states plainly that path planning research for autonomous sailboats remains scarce relative to trajectory tracking and control.

Two structural observations about this literature. First, every learned component predicts wind *in time* at the boat's position; none predicts wind *in space* as a function of geometry — the archipelago problem is absent. Second, none of it learns a world model: the environment model is always hand-built, and learning is confined to the policy or to an auxiliary forecaster. On the classical side, the weather-routing literature (isochrone and DP methods, multi-objective evolutionary extensions, ML-assisted waypoint heuristics — see the 2025 taxonomy review) is mature, but its recent ML hybridization consists of using learned predictors to sharpen cost functions inside the same forecast-given formulation. The forecast-resolution blind spot is inherited, not addressed.

### 2.3 terrain-conditioned wind learning

The strongest external evidence that Claim 2 holds comes from mountain meteorology. Wind-Topo (Dujardin & Lehning 2022) learns to downscale NWP wind to high-resolution topography in the Alps from station observations plus terrain descriptors. DEVINE (Le Toumelin et al. 2023, 2024) trains a CNN to *emulate a CFD model* (ARPS) over thousands of synthetic Gaussian topographies, then transfers to real alpine terrain — reported mean absolute emulation error of 0.16 m/s — and was later coupled with a correction network for operational NWP. TerraWind (Lian et al. 2024) and FuXi-CFD (Lin, Tie et al. 2026) extend the recipe with multi-scale terrain interactions and full 3D reconstruction from coarse forecasts, the latter trained on CFD and recovering latent variables like vertical velocity from horizontal inputs.

The transferable lessons: geometry-conditioned wind structure is learnable by modest networks; training against a *simulator* (CFD) and transferring to reality is a validated strategy — the sim-first curriculum skerry adopts; and the egocentric, rotated-to-wind terrain crop used by DEVINE is, almost detail for detail, the observation encoding skerry uses for chart crops. What this literature lacks is any coupling to control — wind maps are the *product*, not an intermediate representation inside a decision system — and any treatment of the air–water boundary in archipelago planform geometry, where elevation data is sparse and the relevant scale is hundreds of meters, not kilometers.

### 2.4 maritime trajectory learning and data

Deep learning on AIS vessel trajectories is a large, active field — generative transformers over discretized AIS sequences (TrAISformer, Nguyen & Fablet), long-horizon variants, and systematic reviews cataloguing CNN/RNN/transformer pipelines for traffic management and collision risk. A separate thread applies deep RL to USV navigation and COLREG-compliant collision avoidance, typically with hand-specified vessel dynamics (Fossen-style models) and learning confined to the policy; "world model" in the existing maritime literature means a semantic situational-awareness map (Van Baelen et al. 2022), not a learned dynamics model. The relevance here is twofold: AIS corpora are an enormous free source of real trajectory dynamics for pretraining — millions of trajectories, with dense coverage in the confined waters this proposal targets — and the absence of any latent world-model work in the maritime domain confirms the gap from the other side.

A final, practical observation that I have not seen exploited anywhere in this literature: official raster nautical charts (BSB/KAP and similar) are indexed-color images in which the palette *is* a semantic segmentation — land, navigable water, and shallow-water bands are distinct palette entries with full georeferencing in the header. One Swedish harbor chart converts to a clean three-class occupancy grid at ~8.5 m resolution in seconds, with bridges correctly appearing as obstacles. Chart series covering entire national coastlines exist in this format. As a source of *real, navigationally curated* environment geometry for embodied-AI research, this is close to free, and it removes the procedural-generation bias that maze-style benchmarks inherit.

## 3 the gap, stated precisely

Arranging §2 on two axes — *what is learned* and *what domain* — the gap is a single empty cell:

| | environment given | environment learned |
|---|---|---|
| **abstract / indoor navigation** | classical planning | PLDM, DINO-WM, JEPA-WM (§2.1) |
| **marine, confined waters** | routing + sailing RL (§2.2); USV RL (§2.4) | **— (this proposal)** |

with §2.3 sitting off-grid: environment learned, but never used for control. The specific scientific question in the empty cell: **can a latent world model, trained on action-conditioned trajectories in geometry-conditioned wind, internalize the mapping from coastal planform to local wind field well enough that planning over its latents recovers the route choices of an agent with full wind knowledge?** This is a sharper question than "can RL sail a boat" (answered, modulo scale, by §2.2) and different in kind from "can a network downscale wind" (answered by §2.3), because the wind knowledge is never supervised — it must emerge in the latent because it is useful for prediction.

## 4 proposed research program

The full engineering design is specified separately (skerry design document, v0.1); this section states the research-relevant skeleton and the falsifiable milestones.

**Environments from charts.** BSB/KAP charts → palette classification → ink infill → three-class occupancy grids (land / navigable / shallow) at ~8.5 m/cell → an indexed library of 64×64-cell patches (~540 m square) spanning open water, single channels, and multi-island route-choice geometry. Real geometry, near-zero curation cost, held-out charts as the out-of-distribution split.

**A minimal sail simulator.** Deterministic-given-seed, fixed 2 s timestep, discrete heading actions. Boat: polar table, ±40° no-go zone, leeway, a 15 s post-tack speed penalty — the least physics under which upwind strategy is non-trivial. Wind: per-episode base vector composed with precomputed geometry-conditioned shadow and channel-funneling fields (soft downwind shadows with size-proportional decay; divergence-free projection on the water mask for mass conservation), plus an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck gust tier. The wind model is procedural and only plausible; it is the curriculum, not the claim. Its load-bearing property is that wind is a deterministic function of geometry, so there is structure to learn.

**Model and planner.** The PLDM/LeWM recipe at small scale: ViT-Tiny encoder over an egocentric 32×32-cell crop (occupancy one-hot + local wind components) plus scalar state and goal tokens; a ~6-layer action-conditioned AR transformer predictor; JEPA loss against an EMA target with SIGReg regularization; ~12–15M parameters total. Planning by CEM-based MPC in latent space, 60 s horizon, replanned every 10 s — the planner class the JEPA-WM comparison study found robust on navigation-like cost landscapes. Partial observability is deliberate: the agent never sees the global grid or the true wind field.

**Milestones, each falsifiable.** (E1) On geometry alone, kinematic vessel: latent planning must reach ≥90% success and SPL ≥0.7 against an A* oracle — failing this kills the chart-derived-environment premise. (E2) With sailing physics under uniform wind: tacking must *emerge in the planner* from latent rollouts, with no tack ever supervised — failing this kills the claim that JEPA latents capture dynamics constraints of this kind. (E3) The core experiment: under geometry-conditioned wind, an E3-trained model must beat an E2-trained model (sailing-competent, wind-naive) on identical tasks by a significant time-to-goal margin, and a linear probe on its latents must predict the local wind multiplier *on unvisited cells within the observation crop* — together these test whether geometry-implies-wind was internalized rather than memorized per-patch. (E5) The same probes and planning metrics on held-out charts test whether what was learned is the *function* from geometry to wind or the *atlas* of training patches. Negative results at E3 or E5 are reportable: they would locate which ingredient of confined-water structure resists the current JEPA-WM recipe, against a literature (§2.1) that has so far tested these models only where the dynamics field is uniform.

**Benchmark.** A frozen 500-task suite (patch, start, goal, base wind, seed), stratified by geometry class and wind aspect, with sailing-aware A*-oracle baselines, a wind-blind replanning baseline, and a forecast-only isochrone stand-in — published with the grids and the simulator so that any model class can be compared. To my knowledge no benchmark for learned navigation in spatially varying flow fields exists in either the embodied-AI or the maritime literature; the benchmark may outlive the particular model.

**Path to reality, staged and honest.** Phase 2, conditional on E3/E5: pretraining the dynamics prior on AIS trajectories (the §2.4 corpora), fine-tuning on logged sensor data from a real boat through the same episode schema, and calibrating the wind model against paired weather stations spanning known shadow zones — at which point the DEVINE result (CFD-emulator → real terrain transfer) is the precedent for the sim-to-real step. Nothing in phase 1 claims marine-navigation readiness; an advisory aid is the distant target, and the safety posture (suggestion, never authority) is fixed in advance.

## 5 what would falsify the position

Honesty about the failure modes, since a position paper that cannot lose is not saying anything. The position fails if: (a) E3 shows no gap between wind-aware and wind-naive models — implying geometry-conditioned forcing is not extractable from prediction alone at this scale, and the wind-downscaling literature's success does not transfer to the implicit, control-coupled setting; (b) the wind probe succeeds but planning does not improve — implying the latent contains the knowledge but the planner cannot exploit it, an optimization rather than representation failure, which would redirect the program toward value-shaped latents (§2.1) rather than kill it; (c) E5 collapses — implying the model has memorized charts rather than learned physics from geometry, which would demand orders of magnitude more environment diversity than one archipelago provides and would weaken the single-researcher feasibility claim; or (d) the procedural wind field is so far from real archipelago wind that mastery of it predicts nothing — the standing sim-first risk, only retired by the phase-2 calibration study. I consider (d) the most likely partial failure and (b) the most scientifically interesting one.

## 6 conclusion

The claim is narrow and testable: in confined waters, the geometry is the forecast — the information that decides routes is a learnable function of coastline shape, and latent predictive world models are the appropriate learner because what navigation consumes is ranked consequences; the weather map is overhead. The ingredients are unusually ready: a validated small-scale world-model recipe (PLDM/JEPA-WM), external proof that geometry-conditioned wind is learnable (Wind-Topo/DEVINE), free real-world environment geometry hiding in nautical chart palettes, and free trajectory priors in AIS. What is missing is the experiment at the intersection, and it is small enough to run on two consumer GPUs. This paper is the argument for running it.
